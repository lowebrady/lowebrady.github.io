04 — Qualitative UX Research

iPhone vs. Android —
Mobile UX Research Study

A qualitative research study exploring why content creators overwhelmingly prefer iOS over Android — using user interviews, thematic coding in QDA Miner, and pitch-style research communication.

Role Lead Researcher
Methods User Interviews, Thematic Coding, Affinity Mapping
Tools QDA Miner, Google Forms, Figma
Context MSU — Media & Information Research Methods
01 — The Research Question

Why do creators choose iPhone?

The anecdote is everywhere in creator communities: "Switch to iPhone." Android camera specs often match or exceed Apple's on paper — yet content creators, from TikTokers to YouTubers to photographers, consistently gravitate toward iOS. The question isn't whether the preference exists. It's why.

This study set out to understand the underlying UX and ecosystem factors driving that preference — not just the surface-level "better camera" explanation — and translate those insights into design recommendations for mobile platform teams.

"Specs don't tell the full story. Users aren't choosing a sensor — they're choosing a workflow, an ecosystem, and a feeling of creative control. That's a UX problem, not a hardware problem."

02 — Research Design

Qualitative — because numbers alone won't answer this

A survey would tell us what people prefer. Interviews would tell us why. The study used a mixed approach with qualitative depth as the primary driver.

  • 01
    Screener Survey Distributed to identify active content creators with at least 6 months of consistent posting history. Screened for platform diversity (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, photography) and both iOS and Android users.
  • 02
    Semi-Structured Interviews (n=8) 30–45 minute interviews conducted via Zoom. Interview guide covered device history, camera workflow, editing software, cross-device handoff, and frustrations. Participants were encouraged to share their screens and walk through their actual editing process.
  • 03
    Thematic Coding in QDA Miner All interview transcripts were imported into QDA Miner and coded independently, then reconciled. Developed a codebook of 34 codes across 6 parent categories.
  • 04
    Affinity Mapping & Theme Synthesis Grouped codes into emergent themes and built an affinity map to visualize relationships between findings before writing up results.
03 — Key Findings

It's the ecosystem, not just the camera

Three major themes emerged from the data — none of which were simply "the camera is better."

Theme 1

Ecosystem Lock-In as a Feature

iOS users described AirDrop, iCloud Photos, and Continuity Camera as workflow-critical. The seamlessness of Apple-to-Apple handoff was cited more than camera quality by iOS users.

Theme 2

Perceived Consistency of Output

Creators valued predictable, consistent results over peak performance. iPhone's computational photography produces "good enough" results reliably — Android's variability across lighting conditions frustrated creators who needed consistency for branding.

Theme 3

App Ecosystem Priority

Several professional editing apps (CapCut Pro, LumaFusion, specific Lightroom features) launched iOS-first or iOS-only. The app gap was cited by 6/8 participants as a meaningful factor in their decision.

8

Participants Interviewed

5 iOS, 3 Android users — representing Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, and professional photography content creators.

A striking finding: three of the Android users in the study had previously owned iPhones and switched back to Android for hardware reasons — but all three described missing the iOS workflow and app ecosystem.

04 — Design Recommendations

What Android could learn from this

The findings weren't just descriptive — they pointed toward specific, actionable UX improvements for the Android ecosystem. These were presented as formal design recommendations in a pitch-style format.

  • ↳
    Invest in cross-device handoff UX Android's equivalent of AirDrop (Nearby Share) and cross-device sync lacks the "it just works" reliability iOS users described. Smoother Chromebook integration and faster phone-to-tablet handoff would directly address Theme 1.
  • ↳
    Stabilize computational photography output The variability across Android OEMs (Samsung, Google, OnePlus all process differently) is a structural UX problem. Google's Pixel has moved toward consistency — and creators who used Pixel specifically showed fewer complaints about output predictability.
  • ↳
    Partner with creator app developers The app gap is partially a prioritization problem. Google could create a "Creator Tools" program with incentives for day-one Android parity launches for professional content creation apps.
05 — Reflection

Research communication as a UX skill

The most surprising thing I learned on this project wasn't about phones — it was about how much presentation design matters for research findings. The same data, presented as a dense report versus a pitch-style visual deck, generated completely different engagement and questions from the audience.

Good UX research isn't just about collecting and analyzing data. It's about packaging insights in a way that moves stakeholders to action. That's a design problem too — and one I've taken seriously in every project since.
